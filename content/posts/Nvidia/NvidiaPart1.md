> "It's a funny thing about life; if you refuse to accept anything but the best, you very often get it," — W. Somerset Maugham

On the surface, this quote sounds like naive optimism. After all, the real world many times ‘seems’ notoriously indifferent to what you want!

But history shows us a different pattern. Dig a little deeper and you will realise that this quote is quite misunderstood. It isn’t about magic or destiny. It is about the terrifying force of human obsession. When you want something so desperately, that your desire to achieve it completely overrides your ego, you stop playing by the rules and make efforts which are out of ordinary. You live with a level of audacity that forces the world to step back and give you what you want.

But the world was not stepping back for Jensen Huang. In 1997, his startup called Nvidia was exactly 30 days away from insolvency.

Nvidia was halfway through a massive contract to build the NV2 graphics chip for the SEGA gaming console when the tech industry violently shifted beneath their feet. Nvidia had engineered its 3D rendering engine around quadrangles (forward texture mapping) when suddenly, Microsoft launched the DirectX API that crowned triangles (inverse texture mapping) as the undisputed industry standard. Overnight, Nvidia found themselves stuck using the wrong physics and on top of that they were bleeding cash as the rest of the industry raced past them. He was faced with a brutal paradox: fulfill the contract and build a dead product, working in the opposite direction as the industry for two years and eventually go bankrupt; or break the contract, and immediately go bankrupt.

> “Courage is not the absence of fear, but the assessment that something else is more important than fear.” — Franklin D. Roosevelt

Jensen’s assessment was clear: his company’s survival was more important than the fear of humiliation. With no leverage, no capital, and no way out, he flew to Japan to orchestrate one of the most audacious boardroom manoeuvres in tech history.

He sat across SEGA’s CEO Shoichiro Irimajiri, and confessed the brutal truth about Nvidia having the wrong architecture, and as a result, SEGA needing a new partner. Then, Jensen made his ask. He wanted Irimajiri to let Nvidia out of the contract, relieve them of all obligations and still pay them $5 million. It was a completely irrational request, one which would get SEGA absolutely nothing in return.

He exhibited a rare power in admitting total defeat. Sometimes just showing up with absolute honesty commands an almost irrational level of respect; and in some cases it's the only way to secure a ‘yes’.

After considering the ask and looking at the sheer determination and talent in front of him, Irimajiri agreed to help. The $5 million was a lifeline born out of pure grace, and it gave Nvidia just enough runway to hunker down and pivot.

> “A second chance is not a clean slate. It is a heavy, terrifying obligation to build something that cannot be destroyed”
